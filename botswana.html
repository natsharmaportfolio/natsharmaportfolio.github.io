The summer before my junior year, on a trip to Botswana, I was attacked by elephants; the matriarch flipped our car upside down and crushed it. Thankfully we all survived, and I was inspired to figure out a solution to prevent attacks in the future. I worked with the local Botswana camps to design a machine learning program that tracks and predicts elephant migration.

Given a set of satellite images of Botswana collected by DigitalGlobe's WorldView-3 and GeoEye-1 Satellites, my research project implements openCV algorithms to create a machine learning program that tracks elephant herd movement and predicts migration.

The park rangers in Botswana also input the longitude and latitude coordinates of several elephant herds into a spreadsheet. This data is then analyzed and rendered into a heatmap using Tableau's software.

This was the most challenging project I worked on; navigating language barriers, working with teams 9 hours ahead of me on the map, coordinating with park rangers in remote areas of Botswana without service, accessing the satellite's imagery database, learning computer vision using openCV etc. Our project is still ongoing and I hope to see our work overturn the poaching laws in Botswana.

a quick history of what's happening with the elephants in botswana

One of the main reasons the elephants may have attacked our car is because poachers use the same vehicles as tourists. Elephants cannot tell the difference. Poaching is illegal in Botswana, but the camp in Botswana where I was attacked shelters thousands of elephants that have migrated from Namibia, where poaching is legal.

African elephants live between 60-70 years. And in those years they could have experienced hunting and poaching, developing a deep fear of vehicles that look similar to poachers'. Poachers kill elephants and sell their tusks to ivory black markets in Asia.

Machine learning for social impact

Two approaches to the problem

Working with geospatial data presented several challenges, one of which was simply how to approach our machine learning model. We are trying two different methods. The first approach is using latitude and longitude coordinates to build a heat map that displays the movement on an actual map of the area in Botswana (pictured above). And then using openCV functions or computer vision algorithms to isolate and track the movement on the maps. The second approach involves using geospatial coordinates directly in a linear model, without a map.

geospatial Data

Over the past 3 years since the elephant attack, we've collected 25,000 "examples" of data. 1 example of data consists of an elephant's longitude and latitude coordinates, date and time the elephant was spotted by the camp ranger, which emotional state the elephant was in, if there was a body of water present nearby, whether the elephant was alone or in a herd, and the gender. One question we've asked is whether a herd should be count for 1 example of data, or if each elephant in the herd should be individually counted as 1 example of data. We also need more than 25,000 instances. Another problem is that rangers from only 2 camps report elephant locations, versus rangers from every camp in Botswana reporting elephant locations. It would be covering a greater distance and minimizing error in a model's predictions.

Some of the Challenges from this project

We ran into several challenges on this project. The biggest ones were understanding how to incorporate 3 externalities (outliers) into our model: poaching, earthquakes, and decreasing water levels. These 3 points marked big changes in elephant migration pattern.

Poaching

Poaching may have the biggest impact on migration. The reason elephants are leaving Namibia to come to Botswana is because they're escaping poachers. Botswana may legalize poaching too now, so that will have huge implications for migration.

Earthquakes and Tectonic Shifts

3 major earthquakes occurred in Botswana recently, changing the conclusion of our model. A fault line runs through Botswana's delta, where the elephants are. The earthquake caused the water to start flowing east, and elephants are migrating there now. However, far east is several Botswana tribes and villages, where elephants are killed.



water level throughout the year

Instead of summer and winter, Botswana has a wet season and dry season. The rainfall in Angola travels south into Botswana, flooding the delta with water and new wildlife. But recently, Angola's rainfalls were bleak and river water is being used to feed the Angolan population. Botswana is now facing a drought, as water levels drop, the elephant's migration is changing. Many other animals are also changing their paths or going extinct.

Methods and Models

Computer Vision Model for Map

Heatmaps are graphical representations of data in a matrix (pictured below). Using the same openCV framework that I used for my project in Tel Aviv, Israel, I worked with Gil Levi to produce a model that is able to identify instances of elephants and place a new elephant "object" on the map or matrix. We used several OpenCV methods to train our model. cv2.imread() loads our images and reads the image color and grayscale, etc. We trained our model with the DigitalGlobe and USGS Earth satellite images of elephants in Botswana's Okavango Delta. Next, we used another openCV method to get the structuring elements. This was followed by Morphology Transformations (cv2.morphologyEx()), or simply just image filtering. Next our model finds the connected components in the image, and isolates each instance of an object.

The Hungarian algorithm can tell if an object in frame is the same as an object that was previously in frame. Each instance has an ID. This is done using a Convolutional Neural Network. We created an arrayList to track and detect each instance, and stored the info in a matrix. The Kalman Filter algorithm can predict the future position of an instance, for example, after isolating an elephant shadow on a map, the algorithm can place the shadow on a new position in the map. In order to implement the Kalman algorithm, I had to learn more about state mean and covariance. We had to record each instance's position and velocity, and these data points were stored in a matrix. Our conclusion was unclear, as the algorithms give the prediction with a lot of inaccuracy. Overall, the elephants or "dots" on the heatmap are moving east in the openCV models.

Linearity of Geospatial Data

With knowledge in only linear algebra, it was tough to understand how longitude and latitude coordinates could have a true linear relationship, since we want to predict where the elephants will migrate given our negative externalities.

As of now, we only have a basic working computer vision model. What we're working on now is how to use the actual longitude and latitude coordinates, and not just its map visualization of them. With the k-nearest neighbors (KNN) algorithm, we can convert the coordinates into "zones" with a low number of potential clusters, and then assign each instance an ID, and then one-hot encode it. The variables will be converted into a form that could make the algorithm do a better job at predicting. There are several zoning tools for GPS coordinates, such as the automated zoning procedure (AZP) algorithm by Openshaw. Deciding how to manually zone or split regions is also its own task.

The same neural network frameworks that are used to predict traffic on roads and highways can be applied here, if elephants are synonymous with traffic.
